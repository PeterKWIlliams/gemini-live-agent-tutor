### MONEY SMART for Adults: Module 5 – Your Savings

**Module Purpose:**
This module aims to empower individuals to effectively save money for personal goals, significant purchases, and unexpected expenses. It delves into various savings options, highlights the benefits of using financial institutions, and addresses specific strategies for individuals receiving public benefits to build assets responsibly.

---

### Section 1: What is Saving?

**Defining Saving:**
Saving is the intentional act of setting aside a portion of any money earned or received—including income, gifts, and tax refunds—for future use. It emphasizes that consistent saving, even of small amounts, accumulates significantly over time. It's crucial to understand that merely "spending less" is not the same as saving unless the unspent money is actively moved into a savings fund.

**Why Save Money?**
People save money for diverse and important reasons:
* **Achieving Goals:** Funding education, purchasing a home, or other personal aspirations.
* **Building Wealth:** Systematically increasing one's overall financial resources.
* **Emergencies:** Creating a financial safety net for unforeseen circumstances.
* **Covering Income and Expense Fluctuations:** Preparing for periods of reduced income or increased expenses.
* **Peace of Mind:** Reducing financial stress and anxiety.
* **Getting and Keeping a Job:** Having funds for training, job searching, or covering expenses during employment transitions.

**Finding Money to Save (Quick Tips):**
Practical strategies to identify and redirect funds into savings include:
* **ATM Savvy:** Avoiding fees by using in-network ATMs and saving the avoided fees.
* **Bank on It:** Opting for free or low-cost checking accounts and saving any fee reductions.
* **Brand Bias:** Choosing generic or less expensive brands and saving the cost difference.
* **Count Your Coins:** Regularly putting loose change into savings.
* **Direct Deposit:** Arranging for a portion of one's paycheck to be automatically deposited into a savings account.
* **Needs vs. Wants:** Critically evaluating purchases, cutting back on non-essential spending (e.g., frequent dining out, unused subscriptions).
* **Free Fun:** Choosing free entertainment options and saving the money that would have been spent.
* **Goals for Gifts:** Setting gift-giving limits with family/friends and saving part of the usual expense.
* **Shopping Lists:** Sticking to a list to avoid impulse buys and saving the money that would have been spent.
* **"Is it Worth It?":** Calculating the cost of a purchase in terms of hours worked.
* **"Loan to You":** Continuing to "pay" oneself the monthly amount of a recently paid-off loan by putting it into savings.
* **Make it Automatic:** Setting up regular automatic transfers from a checking to a savings account.
* **No Fees:** Paying bills on time to avoid late fees and saving the avoided amounts.
* **Sales Savvy:** Recognizing that a "sale" item isn't always the cheapest; saving the difference if a cheaper alternative is found.
* **Save for the Future (Retirement):** Participating in employer-sponsored retirement plans (especially with employer match) or exploring Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs) for tax advantages.
* **Save Gifts/Tax Refunds:** Saving at least part of any monetary gifts or tax refunds, potentially splitting refunds into multiple accounts.
* **Start Small ("Pay Yourself First"):** Consistently saving small amounts immediately upon receiving income.
* **Take a Pause:** Waiting 24 hours before buying non-essential items and saving a portion if the purchase is ultimately avoided.
* **"Won't Miss It":** Allocating raises or bonuses to savings.

**Key Takeaway (Section 1):** Set aside some money every time you get income. Regularly saving money, even if only a small amount, can make a big difference over time.

### Section 2: Where to Build Your Savings

**Options for Savings (Advantages & Disadvantages):**
Choosing where to keep savings involves weighing various factors:
* **Home (Cash):**
 * *Advantages:* No fees, no rules, convenient, easy access.
 * *Disadvantages:* High risk of loss, theft, or destruction (fire, flood); can be too easy to access, leading to spending.
* **Friend or Family:**
 * *Advantages:* No fees, no costs, potentially convenient, may discourage spending.
 * *Disadvantages:* Risk of loss, theft, or destruction; potential for straining relationships if something goes wrong with the money.
* **Prepaid Card:**
 * *Advantages:* Easy to acquire, electronic/online functionality, convenient, potential for direct deposit.
 * *Disadvantages:* Often involves fees; risk of theft/loss (protection varies by card agreement); may lack federal deposit insurance; difficulty in moving funds to a dedicated savings account; potential expiration dates.
* **Rotating Savings and Credit Association (ROSCA):**
 * *Advantages:* Provides a commitment to group-based saving, discourages easy access, provides a lump sum payout at a scheduled time.
 * *Disadvantages:* Risk of theft/loss due to group mismanagement; may lack federal deposit insurance; risk of members not making required deposits; potential for groups to become too large.
* **Savings Account (at a federally insured financial institution):**
 * *Advantages:* Deposits are federally insured (up to $250,000 by FDIC for banks, NCUA for credit unions); offers direct deposit and automatic transfer options; may earn interest; easy access; helps establish a banking relationship.
 * *Disadvantages:* Potential monthly/recurring fees; may offer lower interest rates for long-term goals compared to other options; accessibility issues for those without nearby branches or online banking; possible limits on withdrawals.

**Other Places for Savings:**
* **Money Market Deposit Accounts:** Typically offered by federally insured financial institutions, these accounts generally provide higher interest rates than standard savings accounts but may require higher minimum balances and have withdrawal limits.
* **Certificates of Deposit (CDs):** Offer higher fixed interest rates in exchange for keeping money untouched for a specific term (e.g., several months to years). Early withdrawals usually incur penalties.
* **U.S. Savings Bonds:** A long-term savings option, purchasable for as little as $25. They have specific holding periods and penalties for early redemption (e.g., losing three months of interest if cashed within five years).
* **Retirement Accounts:** Vehicles like employer-sponsored 401(k)s/403(b)s or Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs) are designed for long-term financial security in retirement. They often offer tax advantages and may include employer matching contributions.
* **Investments (e.g., stocks, corporate bonds, mutual funds):** These options can offer faster growth but also involve a risk of losing some or all of the principal. (This module does not cover investments in detail.)

**Deposit Insurance:**
A significant benefit of saving in federally insured financial institutions is protection for deposits. The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) insures banks, and the National Credit Union Administration (NCUA) insures credit unions, typically up to at least $250,000 per depositor, per institution, per ownership category.

**Interest and Compounding:**
* **Interest:** Money paid by financial institutions to depositors for the use of their money, expressed as a percentage. Interest earned may be subject to income tax.
* **Compounding:** The powerful process of earning interest not only on the initial amount deposited but also on the accumulated interest. More frequent compounding (e.g., daily vs. monthly) leads to greater overall earnings over time.
 * *Example:* $1,000 kept under a mattress remains $1,000. The same $1,000 in a bank account earning 2% interest compounded monthly would grow to $1,105.08 in 5 years and $1,221.10 in 10 years.
 * *Regular Savings Impact:* Combining compounding with regular deposits (e.g., $5/month at 2% compounded monthly) makes savings grow significantly faster. Over 30 years, $1,800 saved would become $2,463.63. Starting to save sooner provides more time for compounding to work its magic.

**Annual Percentage Yield (APY):**
APY is the Annual Percentage Yield, which reflects the total amount of interest earned on a yearly basis, *including* the effect of compounding. It is expressed as a percentage. APY is a more accurate measure than the simple interest rate when comparing potential earnings from different accounts, as it accounts for compounding frequency. The more often interest compounds, the higher the APY and the more interest earned.

**The Rule of 72:**
This is a simple formula used to estimate how long it will take for an investment to double in value. By dividing the number 72 by the annual interest rate (expressed as a whole number), one gets an approximate number of years for the money to double. For example, at a 2% interest rate, money would double in roughly 36 years (72 ÷ 2 = 36). It can also be used to estimate the interest rate needed to double money in a specific number of years (72 ÷ years = interest rate).

**Key Takeaway (Section 2):** Consider the advantages and disadvantages of savings options before choosing where to build your savings.

### Section 3: Saving for Unexpected Expenses

**Why Save for Unexpected Expenses?**
Life is unpredictable, and unexpected events frequently arise, requiring immediate funds (e.g., car repairs, emergency medical/dental work, urgent travel). Having an emergency savings fund is crucial because it allows individuals to cover these costs without incurring expensive debt (e.g., high-interest credit cards or payday loans).

**Emergency Savings Fund Goal:**
While an ideal emergency fund often covers three to six months of living expenses, this can be an intimidating goal. A more attainable and impactful initial goal is to save **$500 to $1,000**. This amount can cover many common unexpected expenses and is a vital first step towards financial health and stability. Building an emergency fund is often a continuous cycle, as funds may need to be replenished after use.

**Anticipating Changes to Income and Expenses:**
Effective financial management requires anticipating fluctuations in income and expenses:
* **Changes to Income:** These can include expected windfalls (tax refunds), unexpected gifts, new jobs with higher pay, acquiring new skills, or taking on additional work. Conversely, income can decrease due to reduced work hours, job loss, or illness/injury.
* **Periodic Bills:** Many expenses are not monthly, such as quarterly auto insurance payments, annual property taxes (if not escrowed), self-employment taxes, or school tuition.
* **Temporary Spending Increases:** Certain times of the year or life events lead to higher temporary expenses, like back-to-school shopping, holidays, cultural celebrations, weddings, summer activities, or unexpected home/appliance repairs.
* *Strategy:* When income temporarily increases or expenses temporarily decrease, consider setting aside a portion of that extra money into savings to better manage future fluctuations.

**Key Takeaway (Section 3):** An emergency savings fund is part of the foundation of financial health. Setting aside $500 to $1,000 can cover many unexpected expenses.

### Section 4: Saving for Your Goals

**Your Hopes and Dreams:**
The initial step in setting financial goals is to reflect on personal aspirations and what one truly desires for oneself and their family. This introspection provides the motivation for goal setting.

**SMART Goals:**
A powerful framework for effective goal setting, SMART goals are:
* **S**pecific: Clearly define what you want to achieve.
* **M**easurable: Quantify your goal (How much? How many?).
* **A**ction-oriented: Identify the concrete steps you will take.
* **R**eachable: Ensure the goal is realistic and attainable.
* **T**ime-bound: Set a specific deadline for achieving the goal.
* *Example:* "I will save $10 each month for six months, by getting cash at my bank's ATM rather than the ATM that charges fees, so that I have $60 for holiday gifts by November 1."

**More Likely to Achieve Your Goals:**
Goals are more likely to be achieved if you:
* Write them down.
* Post them where they are visible daily.
* Share them with others.
* Focus on only one or a few goals at a time.

**How Much Money Should You Save for Your Goals?**
Calculating the amount to save depends on:
* What you are saving for.
* The total cost of the goal.
* How much of that cost you still need to save.
* Your deadline for achieving the goal.
* *Calculation:* To determine a periodic savings amount (daily, weekly, monthly, yearly), divide the *Money Needed* by the *Time to Save*. For instance, to save $1,000 for an emergency fund in two years (approximately 100 weeks), one would need to save $10 per week ($1,000 / 100 weeks = $10/week).

**Large Expenses:**
Large expenses are purchases or costs that typically require more money than one or two paychecks can cover (e.g., a new appliance, a car, education costs).
* **Benefits of Planning:** Thinking about large expenses in advance allows time to save for them, compare prices for the best deal, explore various payment options (using savings, credit), or seek less expensive alternatives.

**Key Takeaway (Section 4):** Create a plan to save money for your goals.

### Section 5: Saving and Public Benefits

**Assets and Income Limits ("Means Testing"):**
Many public benefit programs (e.g., welfare, food assistance, some healthcare) are "means-tested," meaning eligibility is determined by a person's income and other resources (assets). Exceeding these specified income or asset limits can lead to a reduction or loss of benefits.
* **Examples of Asset Limits (as of 2018):**
 * **Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF):** Generally $1,000 to $3,000 in most states.
 * **Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP):** Varies by state.
 * **Medicaid:** Typically $2,000 for single individuals and $3,000 for married couples for certain disability-linked benefits; otherwise, primarily income-tested.
 * **Supplemental Security Income (SSI):** $2,000 for single individuals and $3,000 for married couples.
 * **Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI):** No asset limits (eligibility is based on work history and disability).

**Special Accounts and Public Benefits:**
Certain specialized savings accounts and programs allow eligible individuals to build assets without jeopardizing their eligibility for means-tested public benefits:
* **ABLE Accounts (Achieving a Better Life Experience - 529A):**
 * **Purpose:** Tax-advantaged savings accounts for individuals with significant disabilities that began before their 26th birthday.
 * **Benefit Impact:** Funds in an ABLE account typically do not affect eligibility for Supplemental Security Income (SSI), Medicaid, or other federal means-tested public benefits.
 * **Features:** Anyone can contribute; only one ABLE account per eligible person. Total annual contributions are limited by the federal gift tax exclusion limit (e.g., $15,000 in 2018), though account owners with earned income can contribute more. Balances up to $100,000 allow SSI cash benefits to continue; SSI eligibility is retained but cash benefits are suspended if the balance exceeds $100,000. Total account limits are set by the sponsoring state. Accounts are opened on state program websites, not at banks, and most states accept out-of-state residents.
 * **Qualified Disability Expenses:** Money withdrawn from an ABLE account must be used for "qualified disability expenses" (e.g., education, housing, transportation, employment training, assistive technology, healthcare, financial management, personal support services, or other expenses improving health, independence, or quality of life) to remain tax-free.
* **Special Needs Trust:** A legal arrangement designed to fund the long-term expenses and needs of someone with a disability without impacting their public benefits. These are typically complex and require legal assistance to establish.
* **Pooled Special Needs Trust:** Offers similar benefits to a Special Needs Trust but at a lower cost. A single non-profit entity manages sub-accounts for multiple beneficiaries.
* **Plan to Achieve Self-Support (PASS):** This Social Security Administration (SSA) program allows people with disabilities to set aside money and resources for items or services necessary to achieve a specific education or work goal. The objective is to gain employment (including self-employment) that reduces or eliminates the need for disability benefits. Funds can be used for business supplies, school expenses, equipment, uniforms, transportation, and other items directly related to employment goals.
* **Matched Savings Accounts (e.g., Individual Development Accounts - IDAs, Children's Savings Accounts - CSAs):** These programs, often run by local community-based organizations, encourage saving for specific purposes (e.g., job training, college education, small business start-up, home purchase) by matching the participant's savings contributions. They may require financial education courses and might not count against public benefits if federally funded or part of a PASS.

**Key Takeaway (Section 5):** Some public benefits may be reduced or removed when you exceed income or asset limits. However, some special accounts enable people to save more money without losing eligibility for their benefits.

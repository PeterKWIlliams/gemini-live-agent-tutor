This summary integrates key concepts, definitions, and relationships from both NOAA documents, "Air Quality in a Changing Climate: NOAA’s Role" and "Discover Your Changing World with NOAA," highlighting the critical interplay between climate, air quality, and human activities.

### Part 1: Air Quality in a Changing Climate: NOAA’s Role

The NOAA Science Advisory Board's Climate Working Group report, "Air Quality in a Changing Climate: NOAA’s Role," stresses the urgent need for NOAA to enhance its efforts in observing, understanding, and predicting the intricate interactions and impacts of air quality (AQ) within Earth's changing climate system. It positions air quality not merely as a human health concern with broader ecosystem and agricultural implications, but as a primary pathway through which climate change is directly experienced.

**The Imperative: Air Quality as a Climate Penalty**

NOAA, a leader in climate and Earth system prediction, possesses existing capabilities in AQ research, observation, modeling, and prediction across its OAR, NWS, and NESDIS offices, coordinated by the Climate Program Office (CPO). The report argues that AQ degradation, often termed the "**climate penalty**"—the worsening of air quality due to climate change—must be an integral part of NOAA’s Earth system prediction mandate.

The report examines the close coupling between climate and air quality through three lenses:
1. How a changing climate directly alters air quality.
2. How actions aimed at improving air quality influence climate, and vice versa for climate mitigation.
3. How climate adaptation actions affect air quality.

With the increasing frequency and intensity of extreme climate events (heatwaves, droughts, wildfires), their impacts on air quality, public health, and the economy are becoming more severe. NOAA is uniquely positioned to lead research in this area, focusing on AQ monitoring, understanding, and prediction across various spatial and temporal scales. Key climate factors influencing air quality include stagnation periods, heatwaves, wildland fires, inter-continental transport, desertification/agricultural dust, and urban-rural interactions.

**Key Concepts & Relationships:**

* **Climate Penalty:** The degradation of air quality as a direct consequence of climate change. Warmer temperatures, increased atmospheric stagnation, and altered weather patterns exacerbate ground-level ozone and particulate matter (PM) pollution.
* **Common Origins:** Many air pollutants and climate-forcing agents, especially **Short-Lived Climate Forcers (SLCFs)** like black carbon and methane, originate from the same sources (e.g., fossil fuel combustion). This presents opportunities for "win-win" mitigation strategies that benefit both air quality and climate.
* **Interconnected Impacts:** Climate change impacts air quality through various mechanisms:
 * **Wildfires:** Intensified by droughts and heatwaves, leading to increased smoke emissions and widespread air pollution.
 * **Dust:** Desertification, changes in precipitation, and human land use increase windblown dust, impacting respiratory health and visibility.
 * **Pollen:** Warmer temperatures and altered precipitation patterns can extend pollen seasons, increasing allergic reactions and respiratory issues.
 * **Boundary Layer Dynamics:** The **Planetary Boundary Layer (PBL)**, the lowest part of the atmosphere, governs pollutant mixing and dispersion. Changes in its height and stability under a warming climate significantly affect surface air quality.
 * **Transboundary Transport:** Emissions from distant sources or neighboring countries can significantly influence domestic air quality, requiring international cooperation.
* **Earth System Prediction:** NOAA's core mandate to predict the future state of the planet, which inherently includes air quality as a critical component affecting people, ecosystems, and infrastructure.

**Current State and Gaps in NOAA’s Portfolio:**

NOAA possesses significant capabilities, including observational networks (aircraft, ships, ground stations, NESDIS satellites), process understanding, modeling, and data analysis. However, the report identifies several gaps hindering NOAA’s ability to fully meet its AQ mission in a changing climate:

* Insufficient research on air pollution composition and transport for science and policy needs.
* Inadequate connection between drought research and air quality predictions.
* Poor understanding and modeling of the compounding effects of heatwaves, dust, pollen, and wildfires on AQ.
* Lack of integrated studies on how physical and biological climate-related processes affect air pollution, and poor representation of these in models.
* Inadequate enhancements in boundary layer science, modeling, and observations.
* Insufficient efforts to understand transboundary pollution impacts.
* Limited air quality forecasts (typically 2-3 days), with insufficient research into longer-range (sub-seasonal to seasonal) predictability.
* Lack of groundwork to fully utilize upcoming high-resolution satellite data (e.g., GEO-XO).
* Need for improved forecasting of atmospheric stagnation conditions.
* Sparse observations and predictions in marginalized or poorer communities, which are disproportionately impacted.
* Insufficient research on wintertime ozone and PM formation, and urban-rural interface effects.

**Recommendations:**

The report calls for a workshop to prioritize NOAA's air quality activities and suggests actions across various levels:

**Overarching Issues:**
1. Establish a central **Coordination Office** for NOAA’s air quality research.
2. Provide **sustained funding** for air quality research in a changing climate.
3. Support **early-career researchers** focusing on air quality-climate interactions (drought, heat, wildfire).
4. Enhance coordination with **state and federal agencies**.
5. Develop close working relationships with the **epidemiology community** (NIH, CDC, EPA) for public health alignment.
6. Establish a **Societal Benefits Office** to transition research to applications and engage practitioners.

**Specific Recommendations (summarized):**
* **Climate Extremes and Air Quality:** Leverage existing knowledge (e.g., NIDIS) and establish interdisciplinary observational capabilities for climate-wildfire-AQ coupling.
* **Wildfire, Dust, and Pollen:** Foster collaboration, examine predictability of emission events, develop long-term AQ projections based on drought and weather, and contribute to pollen transport information.
* **Boundary Layer Processes:** Enhance work on atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) dynamics, including emissions, mixing, and forecasting ABL height, and lead an international multi-platform ABL observing system.
* **Observations and Analysis:** Support intercontinental pollution transport studies using new satellite data, develop gold-standard instrumentation, ensure long-term measurements of ozone, and lead aerosol field campaigns.
* **Model Development and Evaluation:** Continuously evaluate operational models, enhance capabilities for multi-day to month AQ forecasts (emissions, chemistry, smoke), and improve treatment of aerosol-cloud interactions in climate models.
* **Air Quality Forecasts:** Test models in hindcasting mode, explore seasonal and ~6-month air quality outlooks leveraging climate phenomena (ENSO, MJO), and consider societal needs from an environmental justice perspective.
* **Stakeholder Engagement:** Develop an integrated paradigm for AQ prediction in a changing climate, collaborating with stakeholders (EPA, USDA, NASA, NIST, DOE) and marginalized communities.

**Appendix Insights (Elaborations):**
The appendix details how the "climate penalty" arises from warmer temperatures, increased stagnation, and altered precipitation. It notes that energy and transportation are common sources of climate-forcing gases and air pollutants, making climate mitigation policies beneficial for air quality. It elaborates on wildfire intensification, the projected increase in **Biogenic Volatile Organic Compounds (BVOCs)** with higher CO2 and temperature, regional changes in desert/agricultural dust, and increasing pollen pollution. It also highlights the crucial role of the **Planetary Boundary Layer (PBL)** in determining pollutant concentrations and discusses NOAA's **Unified Forecast System (UFS)** and other models (GFDL's AM4.1) as critical for developing integrated air quality predictions.

In conclusion, the report asserts that NOAA possesses the mandate and capabilities to significantly advance the nation's understanding and prediction of air quality in a changing climate, vital for meeting diverse stakeholder needs.

### Part 2: Discover Your Changing World with NOAA Activity Book

The "Discover Your Changing World with NOAA" activity book aims to foster **climate literacy**—defined as understanding one's influence on climate and climate's influence on society, enabling informed and responsible decisions. It introduces readers to Earth's climate system, its drivers, impacts, and potential actions, structured around the **7 Essential Principles of Climate Science**.

**The 7 Essential Principles of Climate Science:**
1. The Sun is the primary source of energy for Earth’s climate system.
2. Climate is regulated by complex interactions among components of the Earth system.
3. Life on Earth depends on, is shaped by, and affects climate.
4. Climate varies over space and time through both natural and man-made processes.
5. Our understanding of the climate system is improved through observations, theoretical studies, and modeling.
6. Human activities are impacting the climate system.
7. Climate change will have consequences for the Earth system and human lives.

**Activity Summaries and Key Concepts:**

**Activity 1: The Great Glowing Orb (Making a Solar Heat Engine)**
* **Principle 1: The Sun is the primary source of energy for Earth’s climate system.**
* **Key Concepts:** **Solar Energy** drives Earth's climate. **Heat Transfer** from absorbed solar energy drives atmospheric and oceanic circulation. **Climate vs. Weather** distinction: weather is short-term local conditions, climate is long-term average. **Controls on Earth's Climate** include Earth's shape (equator warmer), tilt and orbit (seasons, Milankovitch Cycles causing ice ages over millennia), and solar output. Earth's overall temperature depends on its **Energy Budget**—the balance between absorbed solar energy and energy released to space.

**Activity 2: The Climate Team (Making a Solar Cooker)**
* **Principle 2: Climate is regulated by complex interactions among components of the Earth system.**
* **Key Concepts:** Earth's "Climate Team" includes the ocean, atmosphere, clouds, ice, land, plants, and animals. The **Ocean** is a major player due to its high **heat capacity** and its **"global conveyor belt"** of currents redistributing heat. The **Atmosphere** exchanges heat and water vapor with the ocean. **Climate Forcings** are components that alter Earth's energy budget. **Clouds** reflect sunlight (cooling) or trap heat (warming), influenced by **aerosols** (tiny particles from natural or human sources). **Ice and Snow** have high **albedo** (reflectivity); their melting exposes darker surfaces, leading to more absorption and further warming (a **positive feedback loop**). All components are interconnected, meaning changes in one part affect the whole system. Plants and animals cycle gases like carbon dioxide.

**Activity 3: Climate Is Our Friend…Isn’t It? (Making an Extinction Polyhedron)**
* **Principle 3: Life on Earth depends on, is shaped by, and affects climate.**
* **Key Concepts:** Life significantly alters Earth's atmosphere over geological time (e.g., the **Oxygen Revolution**). Climate conditions dictate where organisms can thrive; changes can lead to **adaptation, migration, or extinction**. **Fossils** provide evidence of past life and **mass extinction events** (e.g., Cambrian, Permian, Cretaceous), often linked to climate change (global cooling, asteroid impacts). Human activities (habitat destruction, pollution, climate change) are significant drivers of current extinctions.

**Activity 4: Climate, Weather…What’s the Difference? (Making an Electronic Temperature Sensor)**
* **Principle 4: Climate varies over space and time through both natural and man-made processes.**
* **Key Concepts:** Reiteration of **Climate vs. Weather**. Distinction between **Climate Change** (long-term shifts) and **Natural Climate Variability** (year-to-year cycles like El Niño). Evidence shows **Rapid Warming** in the last 50 years, unusual for natural processes, consistent with human-caused increases in greenhouse gases. A **Thermistor** is an electronic component whose **resistance** changes with temperature, useful for continuous measurement. Localized **Microclimates** exist. Temperature is critical for **Water Quality** and aquatic ecosystems.

**Activity 5: How Do We Know? (Making a Home Weather Station)**
* **Principle 5: Our understanding of the climate system is improved through observations, theoretical studies, and modeling.**
* **Key Concepts:** While local weather is chaotic, climate systems follow physical laws, making their long-term behavior predictable. **Environmental Observations** (satellites, buoys, ground stations, and **paleoclimate data** from tree rings, ice cores) are foundational. **Climate Models** combine observations and theories to simulate and predict future conditions. **Model Validation** against past data increases confidence in future projections. Common **Weather Instruments** include a **wind vane** (direction), **Beaufort Scale** (wind speed), **barometer** (**atmospheric pressure**), and **rain gauge**.

**Activity 6: I Didn’t Do It…Did I? (Making Your Own Greenhouse Effect)**
* **Principle 6: Human activities are impacting the climate system.**
* **Key Concepts:** Over 90% **Scientific Consensus** attributes recent warming to human activities, mainly fossil fuel burning. **Greenhouse Gases (GHGs)** (water vapor, CO2, methane) naturally trap heat, essential for life. The **Enhanced Greenhouse Effect** is the human-caused increase in GHG concentrations, primarily CO2 since the Industrial Revolution, leading to more trapped heat and rising global temperatures. Evidence from the **Mauna Loa Curve** and **Ice Core Data** shows a steady, unprecedented rise in atmospheric CO2 correlating with industrialization, far exceeding natural fluctuations (the "**Goldilocks Principle**" contrasts Earth with Venus and Mars).

**Activity 7: Why Should I Care? (Showing How Increased Carbon Dioxide Makes the Ocean More Acidic)**
* **Principle 7: Climate change will have consequences for the Earth system and human lives.**
* **Key Concepts:** Consequences include **Ice Melt & Sea Level Rise** (coastal impacts, freshwater contamination), **Extreme Weather** (heat waves, droughts, floods), and threats to human health and security. **Ocean Acidification** occurs as the ocean absorbs significant anthropogenic CO2, forming carbonic acid, lowering the ocean's **pH**. This severely impacts marine life that build calcium carbonate shells (e.g., oysters, **pteropods**), threatening entire marine food webs and human food sources. The problem is urgent, with severe acidification predicted by 2100 if current emissions continue.

**Activity 8: Are You Climate Literate? (Playing the Essential Principles Challenge)**
* This review activity reinforces the **Essential Principles of Climate Science** and the definition of **Climate Literacy**, emphasizing the impact of global warming, human responsibility, and the need for solutions.

**Activity 9: Communicate! (Creating a Unique Message About Climate Change)**
* Highlights that a **climate-literate person communicates about climate change** effectively. It stresses the **Urgency of Action** by humans and the crucial **Role of Communication** to raise awareness. Since **Energy Consumption & Fossil Fuels** are major GHG sources, reducing energy use is a key solution. The activity encourages **Creative Communication** to engage diverse audiences.

**Activity 10: The Incredible Carbon Journey (Playing the Carbon Journey Game)**
* Illustrates how **human activities have changed the distribution of carbon** in Earth’s spheres.
* **Key Concepts:** **Carbon** is the building block of life. The **Carbon Cycle** describes its natural movement between the **atmosphere, lithosphere, hydrosphere, and biosphere**. CO2 is a crucial **Greenhouse Gas**. Human activities, particularly burning **fossil fuels** (stored lithospheric carbon), rapidly release massive quantities of CO2 into the atmosphere, leading to a significant **Concentration Increase** (nearly 35% for CO2, 150% for methane since the Industrial Revolution) and direct link to global warming. While the total carbon on Earth is constant, humans have dramatically altered its **distribution**.

### Conclusion: Integrated Learning for Climate Literacy

The NOAA activity book provides a comprehensive, hands-on approach to understanding climate science, aligning with the **7 Essential Principles of Climate Science**, Next Generation Science Standards, and Energy Literacy Principles. Its core message emphasizes the complexity of Earth's climate system, driven by solar energy, but now rapidly changing due to human-induced greenhouse gas emissions with significant global consequences. Fostering **climate literacy** is presented as crucial for individuals and communities to make informed decisions and take effective actions to mitigate these impacts and protect our changing world.
